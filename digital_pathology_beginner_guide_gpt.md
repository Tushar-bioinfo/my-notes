# Beginner Digital Pathology Guide for H&E
## Normal tissue, common cell types, and how tumor changes the picture

## What this guide is
This is a beginner-first study guide for learning **how normal H&E tissue looks** and **what usually changes when tissue becomes tumorous**.

It is not a diagnostic manual.  
It is a **visual orientation guide** for learning digital pathology, especially if you are working with:
- H&E whole-slide images
- patch-based review
- tissue recognition
- cell segmentation
- tumor microenvironment reading

Each section gives:
1. what to look for in normal tissue  
2. what usually changes in tumor  
3. a short digital pathology pointer  
4. **3 image links** that are useful for visual learning

---

## How to use this guide
Use the same order every time you look at a slide:

1. **Low power first**
   - Ask: what organ or tissue am I looking at?
   - Look for overall architecture: layers, glands, lobules, ducts, nests, sheets, alveoli, cords, fat, vessels.

2. **Medium power next**
   - Ask: are structures regular or irregular?
   - Look for gland shape, spacing, stromal reaction, invasion front, necrosis, dense cell clusters.

3. **High power last**
   - Ask: what do the nuclei look like?
   - Look for nuclear crowding, pleomorphism, hyperchromasia, nucleoli, mitoses, cell borders, cytoplasm.

---

## Very short H&E rules
- **Hematoxylin** makes nuclei look blue to purple.
- **Eosin** makes cytoplasm, collagen, muscle, and many extracellular parts look pink.
- Normal tissue usually has **order**.
- Tumor usually brings **disorder**.
- Normal tissue usually keeps its expected architecture.
- Tumor often shows **crowding, irregularity, invasion, stromal reaction, and abnormal nuclei**.

# Part 1. Core normal cell types you should learn first

## 1) Squamous epithelial cells / keratinocytes
### Normal H&E look
- Form a layered surface epithelium.
- In skin, they are arranged from basal layer to surface keratin layer.
- Nuclei are denser near the bottom and flatter near the surface.

### In or near tumor
- In squamous tumors, cells often grow in **irregular nests and tongues**.
- You may lose the clean layered pattern.
- Nuclei become more crowded and atypical.
- Keratinization may become abnormal.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
Many models confuse:
- normal keratinized surface
- inflammatory debris
- keratin pearls
- necrotic pink material

## 2) Glandular epithelial cells
### Normal H&E look
- Line glands, ducts, and mucosal surfaces.
- Often appear as neat rings or tubes.
- In colon, glands are straight and evenly spaced.
- Goblet cells can create pale mucin-filled spaces.

### In or near tumor
- Glands become irregular, angulated, back-to-back, fused, or broken apart.
- Cells lose polarity.
- Lumina become distorted.
- Tumor may invade stroma and fat.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
This is one of the biggest patterns in pathology:
- normal gland
- dysplastic gland
- invasive gland-forming carcinoma

## 3) Fibroblasts
### Normal H&E look
- Usually seen as **thin spindle-shaped nuclei** inside connective tissue.
- Cell borders are often hard to see.
- They sit among collagen fibers.

### In or near tumor
- In tumor stroma, fibroblasts can become more active and more obvious.
- They contribute to **desmoplasia**, meaning dense fibrotic tumor stroma.
- You often see more pink collagen and more spindle-shaped stromal nuclei.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
Fibroblast-rich stroma can be mistaken for:
- scar
- reactive tissue
- bland spindle proliferation
- tumor-associated stroma

## 4) Adipocytes
### Normal H&E look
- Big clear round cells.
- Thin rim of cytoplasm.
- Nucleus pushed to the side.
- Often look like empty holes because lipid is removed during processing.

### In or near tumor
- Fat invasion is an important clue in many cancers.
- Tumor cells may appear as irregular glands, nests, or single cells infiltrating between adipocytes.
- Adipocytes themselves usually stay pale and empty, so invading tumor often stands out.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
Fat is easy to recognize, but **tumor invading fat** can be subtle at the border.

## 5) Lymphocytes
### Normal H&E look
- Very small, dark, round nuclei.
- Very little visible cytoplasm.
- Often appear as many tiny blue-purple dots.

### In or near tumor
- Can appear as **tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs)**.
- May cluster at the tumor edge, within stroma, or around necrosis.
- Sometimes they are a good sign of immune response, but the meaning depends on tumor type.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
Lymphocytes strongly affect:
- cell counting
- nucleus segmentation
- TME analysis
- patch labeling

### 3 useful image links

## 6) Plasma cells
### Normal H&E look
- Slightly larger than lymphocytes.
- Eccentric nucleus.
- More cytoplasm than lymphocytes.
- Often collect in mucosa or lymphoid tissue.

### In or near tumor
- May be part of inflammatory infiltrates.
- Can be abundant in reactive stroma.
- In some tumors they sit mixed with lymphocytes and macrophages.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
They can be confused with:
- activated lymphoid cells
- histiocytes
- some tumor cells at low resolution

### 3 useful image links

## 7) Macrophages
### Normal H&E look
- Larger than lymphocytes.
- More cytoplasm.
- Nucleus may be oval, indented, or less dense.
- Cytoplasm can look foamy.

### In or near tumor
- May collect near necrosis, hemorrhage, pigment, or tumor borders.
- In some tumors they become very prominent and can alter the color and texture of the stroma.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
Macrophage-rich regions can look biologically very different from tumor-cell-rich regions.

### 3 useful image links
1. Macrophages, labeled: https://histologyguide.com/slideview/MH-024-026-mesentery/03-slide-1.html  
2. Normal lymph node architecture: https://histologyguide.com/slideview/MH-076-077-078-lymph-node/10-slide-1.html  
3. Lymphoid system chapter: https://histologyguide.com/slidebox/10-lymphoid-system.html

## 8) Endothelial cells and vessel lining
### Normal H&E look
- Very thin, flattened cells lining vessel lumina.
- Nuclei are elongated and delicate.
- Small vessels may look like tiny clear channels with a few flattened nuclei.

### In or near tumor
- Tumor vessels can become irregular, compressed, leaky, or oddly shaped.
- Vascular-rich zones may also contain hemorrhage and inflammatory cells.

### Why this matters for digital pathology
Small vessels are often mistaken for cracks, tissue folds, or empty spaces.

# Part 2. High-yield normal tissue vs tumor pattern map

## 1) Skin / squamous surface epithelium
### Normal H&E
- Thin epidermis on top.
- Dense irregular dermis underneath.
- Clear layered arrangement.
- Hair follicles, sebaceous glands, and sweat glands may be present in thin skin.

### Tumor shift
- Surface architecture becomes disrupted.
- Irregular nests or tongues of tumor invade downward.
- Melanoma instead shows atypical melanocytic growth, often with dermal invasion and atypical nesting or single-cell spread.

### Very short visual pointer
Ask:
- Is the epidermis still layered and orderly?
- Are cells staying where they belong?
- Is something invading the dermis?

### 3 useful image links
1. Normal thin skin with labeled layers: https://histologyguide.com/slideview/MHS-277-278-skin/11-slide-1.html

## 2) Colon / gland-forming mucosa
### Normal H&E
- Straight, evenly spaced crypts.
- Lots of goblet cells.
- Mucosa looks regular and repetitive.
- The overall pattern is highly organized.

### Tumor shift
- Glands become irregular and infiltrative.
- Tumor may form angulated or fused glands.
- Dirty necrosis, desmoplasia, mucin pools, and invasion into deeper layers may appear.

### Very short visual pointer
In normal colon, the pattern repeats.  
In adenocarcinoma, the pattern breaks.

### 3 useful image links
1. Normal colon with labeled crypts: https://histologyguide.com/slideview/MH-123-colon/14-slide-1.html

## 3) Lung / alveolated tissue
### Normal H&E
- Thin alveolar walls.
- Large open air spaces.
- Delicate septa.
- Low-cellularity background compared with many other organs.

### Tumor shift
- Air spaces get replaced by crowded tumor cells.
- In adenocarcinoma you may still see glandular or lepidic-type patterns.
- In squamous carcinoma you often see denser nests and more solid growth.

### Very short visual pointer
Normal lung looks open and airy.  
Tumor lung looks crowded and filled in.

## 4) Breast / ducts and lobules
### Normal H&E
- Ductal and lobular units are relatively organized.
- Stroma and glandular units keep a balanced pattern.
- Luminal spaces and duct outlines remain recognizable.

### Tumor shift
- In invasive ductal carcinoma, malignant glands or cords infiltrate stroma.
- Tubule formation can be reduced.
- Nuclear atypia and mitotic activity increase with grade.

### Very short visual pointer
Look for whether glands are still normal ducts/lobules, or whether they are irregular invasive tubules and sheets.

## 5) Kidney / tubules and glomeruli
### Normal H&E
- Cortex contains glomeruli and many tubules.
- The tissue has a very regular packed tubular appearance.
- Tubules differ in size, cytoplasm, and lumen shape, but the overall arrangement is orderly.

### Tumor shift
- Renal cell carcinoma replaces the normal renal architecture.
- Clear cell RCC often shows pale or clear cytoplasm.
- Vascular pattern may become more obvious.
- Nuclear grade matters.

### Very short visual pointer
Normal kidney looks like repeated tubules plus glomeruli.  
Tumor looks like a new architecture taking over.

## 6) Liver / lobules and plates
### Normal H&E
- Hepatocytes form plates or cords.
- Sinusoids run between them.
- Portal triads and central veins are key landmarks.
- Boundaries between cells can be subtle.

### Tumor shift
- Hepatocellular carcinoma can still resemble hepatocytes, especially in low grade tumors.
- But the normal lobular organization becomes distorted.
- Trabecular, solid, or pseudoglandular growth patterns may appear.

### Very short visual pointer
The hard part is that well-differentiated tumor may still look liver-like.  
So always ask whether the **normal lobule logic** is still present.

## 7) Lymph node / lymphoid tissue
### Normal H&E
- Outer cortex, follicles, paracortex, medulla, and sinuses.
- Highly cellular tissue with many small dark nuclei.
- Architecture is very important.

### Tumor shift
- In lymphoma, the normal compartment structure may be effaced.
- In metastasis, the node may be partly or fully replaced by a non-lymphoid tumor.

### Very short visual pointer
In lymph node pathology, architecture is everything.  
First ask: do I still see a normal node?

## 8) Connective tissue stroma / desmoplasia
### Normal H&E
- Connective tissue is usually relatively low-cellularity compared with tumor.
- You mainly see collagen plus sparse fibroblast nuclei, vessels, and scattered immune cells.

### Tumor shift
- Many invasive carcinomas trigger **desmoplasia**.
- This means fibrotic, reactive stroma around invading tumor.
- The tissue becomes denser, pinker, and more spindle-cell-rich.

### Very short visual pointer
Sometimes the tumor is not the only thing you should segment or classify.  
The surrounding **reactive stroma** also carries information.

# Part 3. Fast tumor-pattern checklist for beginners

## When normal tissue is probably still normal
- Repeating architecture
- Expected layer pattern
- Regular gland spacing
- Stable nuclear size
- Clear tissue boundaries
- No stromal invasion

## When you should suspect tumor or at least strong abnormality
- Crowded nuclei
- Loss of polarity
- Irregular glands or nests
- Invasion into stroma or fat
- Necrosis
- Marked pleomorphism
- Increased mitotic activity
- Architecture no longer matches the organ

# Part 4. What matters most for cell segmentation in H&E tumor slides

## 1) Whole cell borders are often unclear
This is one of the biggest reasons H&E whole-cell segmentation is hard.  
Nuclei are usually easier to see than full cell boundaries.

## 2) Tissue context changes appearance
The same nucleus can look different in:
- inflamed stroma
- necrotic tumor
- dense carcinoma
- mucinous background
- compressed tissue edge

## 3) Tumor microenvironment adds confusion
Fibroblasts, lymphocytes, macrophages, vessels, necrosis, and collagen all change the image texture.

## 4) Some tissues are more “segmentable” than others
Airy tissues, cleaner glandular tissues, and slides with lower crowding are usually easier than dense high-grade tumors.

## 5) Low power and high power both matter
Low power tells you the tissue logic.  
High power tells you the cell logic.

# Part 5. Best beginner study order

1. Skin  
2. Colon  
3. Lung  
4. Breast  
5. Kidney  
6. Liver  
7. Lymph node  
8. Tumor stroma / desmoplasia  

This order is useful because it moves from easier architecture to harder architecture.

---

# Part 6. Very short study method
For each tissue:
1. Look at the normal slide first.
2. Name the main structures out loud.
3. Then open the tumor example.
4. Ask what pattern is lost.
5. Ask what new pattern appears.
6. Write 3 differences only.

That repeated exercise works very well.
