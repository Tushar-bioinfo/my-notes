# The Cell: Molecular Architecture

> [!ABSTRACT] Summary
> Every eukaryotic cell is partitioned into functionally distinct compartments. Each compartment harbors proteins that are mutated, overexpressed, or lost in cancer, and many of these alterations produce morphological changes visible in H&E. Understanding cellular organization is the foundation for reading both histology slides and spatial transcriptomics data.

## Notes

### 1.1 Cellular Organization

#### Plasma Membrane
- Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
- **Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs):** EGFR, HER2, FGFR, VEGFR
- **G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs)**
- **Cell adhesion molecules:** E-cadherin, integrins, selectins
- **MHC class I:** antigen presentation — immune evasion target
- Transporters and ion channels

#### Nucleus
- **Nuclear envelope:** double membrane with pores
- **Chromatin** (DNA + histones)
  - *Euchromatin:* open, transcriptionally active
  - *Heterochromatin:* condensed, transcriptionally silent
- **Nucleolus:** ribosomal RNA synthesis — prominent in cancer cells = active ribosome biogenesis
- Nuclear bodies: Cajal bodies, PML bodies

#### Mitochondria
- Oxidative phosphorylation (ATP production)
- **Apoptosis initiation:** cytochrome c release
- **Warburg effect:** cancer cells shift away from OXPHOS
- Mitochondrial DNA mutations found in some cancers

#### Endoplasmic Reticulum
- **Rough ER:** ribosome-studded, protein synthesis
- **Smooth ER:** lipid synthesis, detoxification
- ER stress → unfolded protein response (UPR) in tumors

#### Golgi Apparatus
- Post-translational modification, sorting, secretion
- Aberrant glycosylation in cancer (altered cell surface)

#### Lysosomes & Autophagosomes
- Degradation of macromolecules
- **Autophagy:** survival mechanism under stress
- Controversial role in cancer: tumor-suppressive vs. tumor-promoting depending on context

#### Cytoskeleton

| Component | Function | Cancer Relevance |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Microfilaments** (actin) | Cell shape, motility | Invasion, migration |
| **Microtubules** (tubulin) | Mitotic spindle, intracellular transport | Chemotherapy target: taxanes |
| **Intermediate filaments** | Structural support | Diagnostic markers |
| → Cytokeratins | Epithelial identity | Important diagnostic marker |
| → Vimentin | Mesenchymal identity | EMT marker |

#### Centrosome
- Microtubule organizing center
- Amplification in cancer → multipolar spindles → chromosomal instability (CIN)

### 1.2 Key Molecular Players

| Molecule Type | Function | Cancer Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **DNA** | Information storage | Mutations, translocations, copy number alterations |
| **RNA** | Information transfer, regulation | Alternative splicing in cancer, miRNA dysregulation, lncRNA in oncogenesis |
| **Proteins** | Effectors of all cellular processes | Mutant proteins with gain/loss of function, overexpression, mislocalization |
| **Lipids** | Membrane structure, signaling | Altered membrane composition, PI3K lipid signaling |
| **Glycans** | Cell surface modification | Aberrant glycosylation is a universal cancer feature |
| **Metabolites** | Energy, building blocks, signaling | Metabolic rewiring (Warburg effect, glutamine addiction) |

### 1.3 Cell-Cell and Cell-Matrix Interactions

Normal tissue architecture depends on adhesive interactions:

#### Epithelial Cell-Cell Junctions

| Junction Type | Key Proteins | Function | Cancer Consequence of Loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tight junctions** | Occludin, claudins | Barrier function | Disruption of polarity |
| **Adherens junctions** | E-cadherin / β-catenin | Cell-cell adhesion | EMT → invasion; β-catenin freed → Wnt signaling |
| **Desmosomes** | Desmoglein, desmocollin | Mechanical attachment | Tissue disintegration |
| **Gap junctions** | Connexins | Intercellular communication | Loss of growth control |

#### Epithelial Cell-Basement Membrane Junctions

| Junction Type | Key Proteins | Function | Cancer Consequence of Loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hemidesmosomes** | Integrin α6β4 → laminin | Anchorage to basement membrane | Detachment → invasion |

#### Basement Membrane Composition
- Type IV collagen, laminin, nidogen, perlecan
- **THE key barrier between in situ and invasive carcinoma**
- Breached during invasion → cancer becomes invasive

> [!TIP] H&E Correlation
> The loss of adhesive structures is directly visible in H&E as the transition from organized, well-demarcated epithelial islands to infiltrative, irregular cell clusters.

## Summary

> [!TIP] Cornell Summary
> The cell is organized into compartments (nucleus, mitochondria, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, cytoskeleton) connected by signaling and structural systems. Cancer hijacks every compartment: nuclear alterations drive mutations, mitochondrial shifts enable the Warburg effect, cytoskeletal changes enable motility, and loss of cell-cell junctions (especially E-cadherin) triggers EMT and invasion. The basement membrane breach is the defining line between in situ and invasive cancer, visible on H&E as loss of organized architecture.
