# Angiogenesis and Metastasis

> [!ABSTRACT] Summary
> Tumor angiogenesis produces structurally defective blood vessels that are leaky, tortuous, and poorly covered by pericytes. These abnormal vessels create a vicious cycle: poor perfusion → hypoxia → HIF-1α → more VEGF → more abnormal vessels. The vasculature anomalies have major clinical implications, including poor drug delivery, hemorrhage, immune suppression, and promotion of metastasis via EMT. H&E reveals characteristic features: irregular dilated vessels, hemorrhagic areas, hemosiderin deposits, and in glioblastoma, glomeruloid microvascular proliferation.

## Notes

### 12.1 Tumor Vessels vs. Normal Vessels

| Feature | Normal Vessel | Tumor Vessel |
|---|---|---|
| **Endothelium** | Tight junctions | Gaps, loose junctions |
| **Pericytes** | Full coverage | Few, loose attachment |
| **Basement membrane** | Intact | Loose, irregular |
| **Lumen** | Regular | Irregular |
| **Blood flow** | Normal | Sluggish, chaotic |

---

### 12.2 Consequences of Abnormal Vasculature

| # | Consequence | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Poor drug delivery** | Leakiness → high interstitial fluid pressure |
| 2 | **Hemorrhage** | Leaky vessels → hemosiderin deposition (yellow-brown on H&E) |
| 3 | **Hypoxia vicious cycle** | Hypoxia → HIF-1α → VEGF → more abnormal vessels → more hypoxia |
| 4 | **Immune suppression** | Hypoxia → glycolytic shift → acidification → T cell/NK dysfunction |
| 5 | **EMT promotion** | Hypoxia → TWIST, SNAIL activation → metastasis |
| 6 | **Increased mutagenesis** | Intermittent flow → reperfusion injury → ROS → DNA damage |

---

### 12.3 Tumor Vasculature on H&E

- Irregular, dilated, thin-walled vessels
- Red blood cells in lumen (or extravasated = hemorrhage)
- Vessels at tumor periphery may be more normal
- **Glomeruloid vessels** (in glioblastoma): tufted, complex vascular proliferations — WHO grade IV feature
- Vascular mimicry: tumor cells form vessel-like channels (melanoma, HCC)

---

### 12.4 Anti-Angiogenic Therapy

| Agent | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Bevacizumab** | VEGF-A | Anti-VEGF antibody, often combined with chemo |
| **Sunitinib** | VEGFR + multi-kinase | RCC, GIST |
| **Sorafenib** | VEGFR + RAF | HCC, RCC |

**Resistance mechanisms:** Alternative pathways (FGF, PDGF), vessel co-option (grow along existing vessels), vascular mimicry

## Summary

> [!TIP] Cornell Summary
> Tumor angiogenesis is driven by the VEGF→HIF-1α axis and produces leaky, tortuous vessels with poor pericyte coverage. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle of hypoxia, immune suppression, and metastatic promotion. Key H&E features include irregular dilated vessels, hemorrhagic areas, and glomeruloid vascular proliferation (diagnostic for glioblastoma). Anti-angiogenics (bevacizumab, sunitinib) are used clinically but resistance develops via alternative pathways, vessel co-option, and vascular mimicry. For computational pathology, microvessel density quantification and spatial vasculature mapping are clinically relevant features.
